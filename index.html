The Al Said Dynasty · 1856 – 1964 · From Stone Town to Revolution

🌟 The Eleven Sultans in Succession

🎭 MAJID
1856–1870
1st Sultan · 1856–1870
Majid bin Said
Reign: 19 Oct 1856 – 7 Oct 1870
Youngest son of Said bin Sultan, became first Sultan of Zanzibar after the division of Oman. Consolidated power around East African trade networks.
⚔️ Survived an attempted usurpation by his brother Barghash (exiled to Bombay).
📷 BARGHASH
2nd Sultan · 1870–1888
Barghash bin Said
Reign: 7 Oct 1870 – 26 Mar 1888
Modernized Stone Town: piped water, electricity, roads, and public buildings. Signed anti-slave trade agreement with Britain and closed the infamous Mkunazini slave market.
🏗️ Grand builder of Zanzibar’s infrastructure and influential Omani-Al Said statesman.
🏛️ KHALIFA I
1888–1890
3rd Sultan · 1888–1890
Khalifa bin Said
Reign: 26 Mar 1888 – 13 Feb 1890
Continued abolitionist policies and maintained close ties with the British. Brief but stable reign of nearly two years.
🕊️ Supported the eradication of slavery; progress towards protectorate status.
🌍 ALI BIN SAID
1890–1893
4th Sultan · 1890–1893
Ali bin Said
Reign: 13 Feb 1890 – 5 Mar 1893
His reign saw the signing of the Heligoland-Zanzibar Treaty (1890), formally establishing Zanzibar as a British protectorate.
📜 Transition to British protectorate under his rule.
⚓ HAMID
1893–1896
5th Sultan · 1893–1896
Hamid bin Thuwaini
Reign: 5 Mar 1893 – 25 Aug 1896
His sudden death triggered the succession crisis that led to the Anglo-Zanzibar War.
⌛ Died unexpectedly, leaving the throne contested.
⚡ KHALID BIN BARGHASH
2 DAYS
6th Sultan · 1896 (38 minutes war)
Khalid bin Barghash
Reign: 25–27 August 1896 (2 days)
Seized the palace after Hamid’s death. Refused British ultimatum to step down, leading to the Anglo-Zanzibar War — the shortest war in history (38 minutes). Deposed and exiled.
🔥 The shortest war in recorded history: 38 minutes of naval bombardment.
📜 HAMOUD
1896–1902
7th Sultan · 1896–1902
Hamoud bin Mohammed
Reign: 27 Aug 1896 – 18 Jul 1902
Issued the final decree abolishing slavery in Zanzibar (6 April 1897). Knighted by Queen Victoria for his reforms. Pro-British and progressive.
🏅 Abolished slavery entirely — a turning point in Zanzibari society.
🎓 ALI BIN HAMUD
1902–1911
8th Sultan · 1902–1911
Ali bin Hamud
Reign: 20 Jul 1902 – 9 Dec 1911
Ascended as a child; British regent ruled until he came of age. Later abdicated to live in Europe.
✈️ Abdicated after attending the coronation of King George V.
👑 KHALIFA II
9th Sultan · 1911–1960 (Longest reign)
Khalifa bin Harub
Reign: 9 Dec 1911 – 9 Oct 1960
Reigned nearly 49 years. Oversaw construction of modern harbor in Stone Town, tar roads, and institutional development. A highly respected figure during the late British protectorate.
🌟 Guided Zanzibar through two world wars and early independence movements.
🌴 ABDULLAH
1960–1963
10th Sultan · 1960–1963
Abdullah bin Khalifa
Reign: 9 Oct 1960 – 1 Jul 1963
Son of Khalifa bin Harub. His reign was short due to health issues; died from complications of diabetes.
🕊️ Passed away just months before Zanzibar’s independence from Britain.
🍂 JAMSHID
1963–1964
11th Sultan · 1963–1964 (overthrown)
Jamshid bin Abdullah
Reign: 1 Jul 1963 – 12 Jan 1964
Oversaw Zanzibar’s independence from Britain on 10 December 1963 as a constitutional monarchy. Overthrown just one month later during the Zanzibar Revolution (12 January 1964). Fled into exile in the United Kingdom.
📅 Last Sultan of Zanzibar; the monarchy was replaced by the People’s Republic.

38-minute war & Hamoud’s abolition decree (1897).
1963

🏝️ Legacy of the Al Said Sultans

Stone Town’s Architecture

The Sultans left an indelible mark on Stone Town — coral stone mansions, carved doors, the House of Wonders, and the Sultan's Palace (now a museum). Their patronage fused Swahili, Arab, Persian, and Indian styles.

Abolition of Slavery

Under British pressure and reformist sultans like Barghash and Hamoud, Zanzibar became a hub for the abolition movement in East Africa. The 1897 decree marked a fundamental shift.

Cultural Fusion

The royal court patronized taarab music, Swahili literature, and international trade, creating a cosmopolitan identity that still resonates across the Indian Ocean.
